# Jacob, Esau, and Rebekah — Primary Lesson Plan
*Come Follow Me Week 10 | Genesis 24-33 | "Let God Prevail"*
*Prepared: 2026-03-08 | Ages 3-11*

---

## Context

This week's readings cover Genesis 24-33, the stories of Isaac and Rebekah, the birth and rivalry of Jacob and Esau, Jacob's deceptive claiming of the birthright blessing, and his vision of the ladder reaching to heaven. These stories are messy and wonderfully human — full of favoritism, deception, hurt feelings, and yet unmistakably threaded through with God's patient, continuing plan.

For Primary, the goal is not to resolve every moral tension (Genesis 27 is genuinely difficult) but to focus on what God is doing, what choices cost us, and how He reaches out to us even when we are far from home or have made mistakes.

**CFM Week Title:** "Let God Prevail"
**Core Gospel Thread:** God has a plan for each of us. He calls us by name, guides our families, and never stops reaching out — even when we run away.

---

## Key Themes

- God guides families and has a plan that spans generations
- The birthright and priesthood covenants matter — spiritual things are more valuable than physical things
- Choices have real consequences for ourselves and the people we love
- God reaches out to us even when we are alone, afraid, or far from home (Jacob's ladder)
- Honesty and integrity keep families strong
- Rebekah showed courage and faith in leaving home to be part of God's covenant family

---

## Scripture Highlights for Teaching

### Rebekah at the Well (Genesis 24:15-20, 58)
Rebekah drew water for ten thirsty camels — a lot of hard work — showing kindness before she knew who was watching. When asked if she would go with the servant to marry Isaac, she said simply: "I will go." Courage and willingness.

### The Birth of Jacob and Esau (Genesis 25:21-26)
Rebekah prayed to understand why the twins were struggling. God answered her directly and told her these two boys would become two great peoples. Even before they were born, God knew them and had a plan.

### Esau Sells His Birthright (Genesis 25:29-34)
Esau came home hungry from hunting and traded his birthright — his special spiritual inheritance — for a bowl of soup. He was so focused on what he wanted right now that he forgot what mattered most. The scripture says plainly: "Esau despised his birthright."

### Jacob Receives the Blessing (Genesis 27)
Jacob and Rebekah deceive Isaac. This is the hardest passage for children because both Jacob and his mother do something wrong. Acknowledge it honestly: deception hurt Esau deeply, and it forced Jacob to flee his home and never see his mother again. Choices have consequences. But God's plan continued moving forward, which shows His patience with imperfect people.

### Jacob's Ladder (Genesis 28:10-17)
Alone, in the dark, sleeping on a rock, far from home — Jacob dreamed of a ladder reaching to heaven with angels going up and down. The Lord spoke to him: "I am with thee, and will keep thee in all places whither thou goest." Jacob woke up and said, "Surely the LORD is in this place; and I knew it not." God finds us even when we feel most alone.

## Lesson Plan

### Opening (5 minutes)

**Opening Song:** "Families Can Be Together Forever" (CS, 188) or "I Am a Child of God"

**Opening Question (hold up a bowl):** "Has anyone ever wanted something SO much that they would trade almost anything to get it? What was it?"

Let a few children share. Then say: "Today we are going to learn about a man who was so hungry that he traded something incredibly special — and later realized what a mistake that was. We are also going to learn about a dream that showed someone God was watching over him even when he felt all alone."

---

### Story Block 1: Rebekah — A Brave and Kind Girl (5-7 minutes)

**Tell the story in your own words:**

A long time ago, a servant traveled far across the desert with ten camels. He stopped at a well and prayed: "Heavenly Father, please help me find the right wife for Isaac. Let the girl who gives water not just to me but also to my camels — let her be the one."

Before he even finished praying, Rebekah came to the well. She gave him water and then offered — all on her own — to draw water for all ten thirsty camels too. Drawing water for ten camels is a lot of hard work! The servant knew his prayer had been answered.

Later, Rebekah's family asked her: "Will you go with this stranger to marry a man you have never met?" She said, "I will go." She trusted God had a plan.

**Discussion Questions:**
- What did Rebekah do that was kind? (Gave water, worked hard without being asked)
- Why do you think she said "I will go" so bravely?
- Can you think of a time you chose to be kind when nobody was watching?

---

### Story Block 2: Two Brothers, One Very Bad Trade (7-10 minutes)

**Tell the story:**

Isaac and Rebekah had twin boys named Esau and Jacob. Esau came out first, which made him the "firstborn." In those days, the firstborn son received something called the "birthright" — a special blessing and responsibility to lead the family in following God's covenants. It was incredibly important, like being given the most precious treasure in the family.

Esau loved hunting. Jacob liked to stay home and cook. One day Esau came home from a long hunting trip feeling very hungry. Jacob was cooking a pot of red soup that smelled amazing.

Esau said: "Give me some of that soup, I'm starving!"
Jacob said: "I'll trade you — give me your birthright."
Esau said: "What good is a birthright if I'm starving? Fine, take it."

So Jacob gave him soup, and Esau gave away something priceless for something he could have gotten anywhere.

Later, their father Isaac was old and nearly blind. He asked Esau to hunt and prepare food so he could give him the big family blessing. But Rebekah and Jacob came up with a plan to trick Isaac into giving the blessing to Jacob instead. Jacob dressed in Esau's clothes and put goat skin on his arms so he would feel hairy like his brother. Isaac was confused but gave Jacob the blessing.

When Esau found out, he was so upset and hurt that he cried. Jacob had to run away from home, and things in the family were broken.

**Discussion Questions:**
- Was Esau's trade a good trade? What did he give up? What did he get?
- Was Jacob and Rebekah's plan a good plan, even if Jacob ended up with the blessing?
- Can you think of a time a choice someone made hurt someone else's feelings?

---

### Story Block 3: Jacob's Ladder — God Finds Us (7-10 minutes)

**Tell the story:**

After Jacob ran away from home, he was all alone. It was dark. He had no tent, no bed — just some rocks. He put a rock under his head like a pillow and fell asleep.

And then something amazing happened.

He dreamed he saw a ladder — the most beautiful, tall ladder you could ever imagine — stretching all the way from the ground up to heaven. Angels were going up and down on it. And at the top, God was there.

And God said: "I am the Lord. I am with you. I will watch over you everywhere you go, and I will bring you home."

Jacob woke up and said: "God was in this place — and I didn't even know it!"

He took the stone that had been his pillow, stood it up, and named that place "Beth-el" — which means "House of God." He made a promise to God right there.

**Discussion Questions:**
- Jacob was alone, scared, sleeping on rocks. How do you think that felt?
- Why do you think God chose that moment to talk to Jacob?
- Has Heavenly Father ever helped you feel better when you were scared or sad?
- What does it mean to you that God said, "I am with you everywhere you go"?

## Key Gospel Principles for Children

1. **God knows us before we are born** (Rebekah's experience, Genesis 25:23) — Heavenly Father knows each of us and has a plan for our lives.

2. **Spiritual things are more important than physical things** — A bowl of soup satisfied Esau for a few hours. The birthright and its covenants could bless his whole eternal family. Some things matter a lot more than they look.

3. **Choices have real consequences** — Jacob and Rebekah's deception broke something in the family. Jacob had to leave. Esau was heartbroken. We should always try to be honest, even when it is hard.

4. **God doesn't give up on us** — Even after all the mistakes in Jacob's family, God appeared to Jacob and renewed the covenant. He still had a plan. Our Heavenly Father is incredibly patient.

5. **God is with us when we feel alone** — Jacob's ladder reminds us that heaven is not far away. God is always watching, always near, always ready to help.

## Object Lessons

### Object Lesson 1: The Soup Trade
**Materials:** A bowl of soup or crackers, and a piece of paper that says "ETERNAL BLESSING" in big letters.

Hold up the bowl in one hand and the paper in the other. Ask: "Which one would you trade for which?" Then say: "Sometimes things that feel very important right now — I'm hungry, I'm tired, I want that thing — make us forget about things that matter forever. Esau made that mistake. We can learn from it."

For younger children (ages 3-6): Simply say "Esau traded something very special for something that only lasted one day. We want to make choices that last forever."

### Object Lesson 2: Sleeping on Rocks / Jacob's Pillow
**Materials:** A smooth rock (safe for children to hold)

Pass the rock around and let each child feel it. Say: "This is what Jacob used as a pillow the night he had the dream about the ladder. Imagine sleeping on this! He was alone, far from home, probably scared. And that was the night God chose to send him a dream and say, 'I am with you.'"

Ask: "Why do you think God might especially want to talk to us when things are hard?"

For younger children: Let them hold the rock and say, "Even when things feel hard and rocky, God is close."

### Object Lesson 3: The Ladder
**Materials:** Draw a simple ladder on a piece of paper, or bring a small toy ladder

Hold up the ladder. Say: "Jacob saw a ladder from earth all the way to heaven. Angels were going up and down. You know what that means? Heaven and earth are connected. Heavenly Father can hear every prayer, every time. He is never too far away to help."

For older children (ages 8-11): Ask "What do you think the ladder might mean as a symbol? What connects us to heaven?" (Prayer, scripture, prophets, the Holy Ghost, covenants, temples)

## Activities

### Activity 1: The Birthright vs. the Soup (Ages 4-10)
**What you need:** Index cards or slips of paper

Give each child two cards. On one they draw or write something they want RIGHT NOW (candy, a toy, to play video games). On the other they draw or write something that matters forever (their family, the gospel, baptism, the temple).

Have them hold one in each hand. Ask: "If you had to trade one for the other, which would you choose?"

Discuss briefly: "Esau chose the thing he wanted right now. When we are making choices, it helps to ask: 'Does this choice help me get the things that last forever?'"

### Activity 2: Rebekah's Kindness Challenge (Ages 3-8)
Before the lesson, prepare a simple "Well" — a bucket or basket in the center of the room with small cups of water or slips of paper saying "water."

Pick one child to be the "servant" and one to be Rebekah. Have the servant ask, "May I have a drink?" and have Rebekah offer water plus extra (for the "camels"). Then tell all the children: "Rebekah did more than she was asked. This week, your challenge is to do one kind thing that nobody asked you to do. Can you do that?"

For younger children: Have all the children say together: "I will be kind even when nobody is watching!"

### Activity 3: Jacob's Ladder Drawing (Ages 4-11)
Give each child a blank piece of paper and crayons. Ask them to draw Jacob's ladder. At the top, they write or draw "Heavenly Father." On the ladder rungs, they write or draw things that help us feel close to God: prayer, scriptures, church, family, kindness, repentance.

Let older children (8-11) write a few sentences about a time they felt God was close to them.

Younger children can just draw angels on the ladder and color the sky.

## Visual and Storytelling Ideas

### Flannel Board or Pictures
Use Gospel Art Book images:
- "Rebekah at the Well" (if available, or draw a simple well scene)
- "Jacob's Dream" (Gospel Art Kit 121)
- Simple family tree showing Abraham > Isaac and Rebekah > Jacob and Esau

### Storytelling Technique: "Freeze Frame"
As you tell the story, pause at dramatic moments and ask kids to "freeze" and make a face showing how the character feels:
- "Freeze — show me Esau's face when he smelled Jacob's soup"
- "Freeze — show me Jacob's face when he woke up from the dream"
- "Freeze — show me Rebekah's face when they asked 'will you go?' and she decided to be brave"

This helps younger children stay engaged and connects emotion to the story.

### Map Visual
Show a simple hand-drawn map: Start at "home" (Beer-sheba). Draw an arrow to a lonely spot in the desert where Jacob slept. Draw a ladder going up. Then an arrow going toward "Haran." Tell the kids Jacob was far from home and family when God visited him — and that made the message even more special.

## Age-Appropriate Applications

### Ages 3-5
- "God loves you and knows your name, just like He knew Jacob before he was even born."
- "When you feel scared at night, you can say a prayer just like Jacob did, and Heavenly Father will hear you."
- "Rebekah was kind and helpful. How can you be kind today?"

### Ages 6-8
- "Esau made a quick trade without thinking about what he was giving up. When you have a big choice, it helps to slow down and ask, 'Will I be glad I made this choice tomorrow? Next year? When I am old?'"
- "Jacob made a mistake by not being honest. Even though things worked out in God's plan, Jacob had to deal with hard consequences. Honesty is always better."
- "God told Jacob, 'I am with you everywhere you go.' He says the same thing to you through the Holy Ghost."

### Ages 9-11
- "These stories show us that God can work through imperfect families and imperfect people. He doesn't wait for us to be perfect before He loves us or gives us a mission."
- "The birthright carried the responsibility of priesthood covenants and blessings for the whole family. What covenants do you have, or are you working toward, that are like a spiritual birthright?"
- "Jacob's ladder points to the temple — a place where heaven and earth meet. What can you do to prepare to make covenants in the temple?"

## Closing Testimony Moment

Before the closing prayer, read Genesis 28:15 slowly:

> "Behold, I am with thee, and will keep thee in all places whither thou goest..."

Ask: "Has anyone ever felt like Jacob — maybe a little alone, or a little scared, or far from where they wanted to be? The same God who sent a dream to Jacob on that dark night is watching over each of you. He knows your name. He has a plan for you. He is never as far away as it might feel."

Bear your testimony of God's personal love for each child.

**Closing Song:** "I Know My Father Lives" (CS, 5) or "A Child's Prayer"

## Teacher Notes and Preparation

### Handling Genesis 27 Honestly
Children will notice that Jacob lies. Don't skip over it or paper it over. A simple honest framing:

"Jacob and his mother Rebekah made a choice that wasn't honest, and it caused a lot of hurt. Esau cried. Jacob had to run away from home for a long time and never saw his mom again. Even when things eventually worked out in God's plan, the deception cost their family something real. We can love these people in our scriptures and still learn from their mistakes."

### The Complexity of Rebekah
The scripture tells us "Rebekah loved Jacob" (Genesis 25:28) in a way that showed favoritism, which contributed to the family's problems. For older children, you can briefly acknowledge that parents are human and sometimes make mistakes too. The lesson isn't to judge Rebekah but to note that family harmony is worth protecting.

### For the Youngest Children (Sunbeams, 3-4)
Keep it to two simple truths:
1. God knows us and loves us.
2. Jacob saw a ladder to heaven and learned God was always close.

Act out the ladder dream together: have children lie down on the floor, close their eyes, then "wake up" surprised and happy. Say: "Jacob woke up and said, 'God was HERE and I didn't even know it!' That is so wonderful."

## Questions to Ponder (For Personal Teacher Preparation)

- When have I felt like Jacob — alone, far from home, sleeping on rocks — and been surprised to find God near?
- Are there "Esau trades" I make — giving up spiritual priorities for immediate comfort or convenience?
- How does Rebekah's courage ("I will go") inspire me in my own covenant commitments?
- Jacob's ladder is often connected to the temple. How does the temple help me feel that connection between earth and heaven?
