# Review: Elder Rasband & President Oaks — October 2025 General Conference

Two apostolic talks on the family, delivered at the same conference — one defending the Family Proclamation on its 30th anniversary, the other framing the entire gospel as family-centered. Together they form a powerful statement on the doctrine of the family.

---

## 1. "The Family Proclamation — Words from God" — Elder Ronald A. Rasband

**Session**: Saturday Afternoon
**Link**: [churchofjesuschrist.org](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/general-conference/2025/10/21rasband?lang=eng)

### Context

October 2025 marked the 30th anniversary of "The Family: A Proclamation to the World." Elder Rasband used this milestone to reaffirm the proclamation's divine origin and continuing relevance. He shared that in 1994 — a year before it was presented — the Quorum of the Twelve discussed how society and governments were pulling away from God's laws for family, marriage, and gender. The apostles who prepared the proclamation were "seers called of God."

When Elder Rasband was called to the apostleship in 2015, he was told: "This proclamation is now yours. Your name is right here" — pointing to the words "Council of the Twelve Apostles" in the document's heading.

### Key Themes

- **Divine origin**: "The proclamation has a divine origin, so we must treat it with the reverence deserving words from God." It is doctrine, as President Hinckley stated in 1995.
- **Divine identity**: Every person is "a beloved spirit son or daughter of heavenly parents... [with] a divine nature and destiny."
- **Equal partnership**: "Fathers and mothers are obligated to help one another as equal partners." He emphasized that *preside* does not mean dominate and *nurture* does not mean secondary. He shared how Sister Rasband once corrected him for making a decision without her, shaping their commitment to equality.
- **Compassion for those who struggle**: "Stay with us. You live in challenging times when the adversary seeks to make you his. Do not be drawn off. And if you are, come back." Christ Himself offers strength and belonging.
- **Family happiness**: "Happiness in family life is most likely to be achieved when founded upon the teachings of Jesus Christ."

### Key Quotes

> "The principles are not out of step, but perfectly in step with the ways of the Lord and His covenant path."

> "You have family in your Savior, Jesus Christ, who loves you. He came to earth to atone for our sins and bear the burden of our mistakes and our very bad days."

> "Our Father in Heaven has provided the proclamation on the family to help guide us home to Him."

### Scriptures Referenced
- The Family: A Proclamation to the World (1995)

## 2. "The Family-Centered Gospel of Jesus Christ" — President Dallin H. Oaks

**Session**: Sunday Afternoon (Closing Talk)
**Link**: [churchofjesuschrist.org](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/general-conference/2025/10/58oaks?lang=eng)

### Context

President Oaks closed the October 2025 general conference — just days before being set apart as the 18th President of the Church on October 14, 2025, following President Nelson's passing. His address affirmed that the gospel plan centers on the family and addressed contemporary concerns about declining marriage and birthrates.

### Key Themes

- **Family is central to God's plan**: "The gospel plan was first taught to us in the council of an eternal family, it is implemented through our mortal families, and its intended destiny is to exalt the children of God in eternal families."
- **Temple ordinances enable eternal families**: Temples are essential — ordinances received there enable members to return as eternal families.
- **Declining marriage and birthrates**: The U.S. has suffered deteriorating marriage and childbearing rates for nearly a century. Church members' rates, while more positive, have also declined significantly. He stressed it is vital not to lose understanding of the purpose of marriage and the value of children.
- **Practical family strengthening**:
  - Pray together, kneeling night and morning
  - Worship together in Church services
  - Share family stories, traditions, and sacred experiences
  - Do meaningful activities together (gardens, camping, sports, reunions)
  - Teach children basic life skills
  - **Turn off technology**: "Many parents will find that they can turn their family on if they all turn their technologies off. And parents remember, what those children really want for dinner is time with you."
- **Non-traditional families**: Shared how his grandfather said "I will be your father" after President Oaks' father died — a powerful example of filling gaps when families are incomplete.
- **Love for those who struggle**: Family members who don't embrace gospel values need love and patience. The perfection we seek is not limited to mortality — repentance and growth continue in the spirit world.
- **Christlike service as remedy**: "Following Christ and giving ourselves in service to one another is the best remedy for the selfishness and individualism that now seem to be so common."

### Key Quotes

> "The gospel plan was first taught to us in the council of an eternal family, it is implemented through our mortal families, and its intended destiny is to exalt the children of God in eternal families."

> "Many parents will find that they can turn their family on if they all turn their technologies off."

> "I will be your father." — President Oaks' grandfather, after his father died

### Scriptures Referenced
- Doctrine and Covenants 131-132 (temple sealing, eternal marriage)
- The Family: A Proclamation to the World

## Common Themes Across Both Talks

### 1. The Family as God's Central Unit
Elder Rasband defended the Family Proclamation as doctrine with divine origin. President Oaks went further, framing the *entire gospel plan* in family terms — taught in an eternal family, implemented through mortal families, destined for eternal families. Together they present the family not as one topic among many, but as the organizing principle of the plan of salvation.

### 2. The Family Proclamation as Living Doctrine
Both talks reference the proclamation directly. Elder Rasband calls it "words from God" and doctrine; President Oaks builds on it to teach about family-centered worship. On the proclamation's 30th anniversary, the message is clear: this is not a relic of the 1990s — it is urgent, current counsel.

### 3. Compassion Without Compromise
Both speakers hold firm on doctrine while extending deep compassion. Elder Rasband: "Stay with us... come back." President Oaks: repentance and growth continue beyond mortality, and those who struggle need love and patience. Neither speaker softens the doctrine, but both soften the delivery.

### 4. Equal Partnership and Mutual Service
Elder Rasband explicitly addressed equal partnership in marriage — preside does not mean dominate, nurture does not mean secondary. President Oaks taught that Christlike service to one another is the antidote to selfishness. Both envision family relationships defined by mutual sacrifice, not hierarchy.

### 5. Practical, Not Just Doctrinal
President Oaks offered strikingly concrete counsel — turn off technology, pray kneeling together, do yard work with your kids, hold family reunions. Elder Rasband shared his own marriage as a model of working through disagreement. These are not abstract sermons; they include things you can do *this week*.

## Parallels

### Programming
President Oaks' counsel to "turn off technology" is ironic coming from a Church that invests heavily in technology — but it maps to a principle every developer knows: **the tool serves the purpose, not the other way around.** Technology is a means to connect, but when the tool becomes the default mode of interaction, it displaces the real thing. It's like writing abstraction layers so thick that you lose sight of the actual data underneath. Sometimes the best engineering decision is to remove a layer, not add one.

### Tolkien
Elder Rasband's plea — "Stay with us... come back" — echoes the mercy shown throughout Tolkien. Gandalf's insistence on mercy toward Gollum, Frodo's refusal to kill Saruman, Aragorn's welcome of the Oathbreakers who had once betrayed their covenant. The gospel pattern is the same: the door remains open for those who have wandered, and returning is always honored.

President Oaks' grandfather saying "I will be your father" echoes the way fellowship works in Tolkien — Aragorn, Gandalf, and others become family to the hobbits not by blood but by covenant and choice. The Fellowship of the Ring is itself a chosen family bound by a shared mission, just as President Oaks describes families that fill in gaps for one another.

### Literature
"I will be your father" carries the weight of a covenant moment in literature — it echoes adoption narratives like Jean Valjean taking in Cosette in *Les Miserables*, or Atticus Finch's quiet, steady fatherhood in *To Kill a Mockingbird*. One person's commitment to fill a gap reshapes a life entirely.

### Anime
Elder Rasband's "Stay with us... come back" has the energy of Naruto's refusal to give up on Sasuke. Despite Sasuke's rebellion and wandering, Naruto never stops holding a place for him. The bond of chosen brotherhood — nakama — mirrors the gospel invitation: we don't disown people who struggle. We hold their place and welcome them back.

## Deeper Themes from the Synthesis

### Theme 1: The Family Is the Grammar of the Gospel

President Oaks' formulation is striking in its completeness: the gospel was *taught* in an eternal family (premortal council), *implemented* through mortal families, and *destined* to produce eternal families. This means family is not just a topic the gospel addresses — it is the **structure** through which the gospel operates. Every ordinance, every covenant, every doctrine ultimately connects back to family relationships. The proclamation that Elder Rasband defends is not a policy statement; it's a description of reality's architecture.

**Cross-references**:
- Moses 1:39 — "This is my work and my glory — to bring to pass the immortality and eternal life of man." If God's work is exaltation, and exaltation requires eternal family (D&C 131:1-4), then family *is* the mechanism of God's work.
- D&C 2:2 — Elijah will "plant in the hearts of the children the promises made to the fathers." The very last prophecy of the Old Testament (Malachi 4:5-6) and the first revelation of the Restoration (D&C 2) are both about turning hearts within families. The first and last word is family.

### Theme 2: Doctrine with Open Arms

Both speakers model a posture that is rare and worth studying: firm on doctrine, warm in delivery. Elder Rasband calls the proclamation "doctrine" and "words from God" — no hedging. Then in the same talk: "Stay with us... come back." President Oaks affirms that marriage and family are central to exaltation, then immediately adds that perfection is not limited to mortality and that those who struggle deserve love and patience.

This is not contradiction — it is the full picture. Truth without mercy is cruelty. Mercy without truth is sentimentality. Both speakers hold the tension.

**Cross-references**:
- Alma 42:25 — "What, do ye suppose that mercy can rob justice? I say unto you, Nay; not one whit. If so, God would cease to be God." Alma teaches Corianton the same dual reality: justice is real, mercy is real, and neither cancels the other.
- John 8:11 — "Neither do I condemn thee: go, and sin no more." Christ's response to the woman taken in adultery: compassion first, then the standard.

### Theme 3: The Grandfather Principle — Filling the Gaps

President Oaks' grandfather saying "I will be your father" may be the most quietly powerful moment across both talks. It reveals something about how God's plan accounts for brokenness: not by making every family perfect from the start, but by providing people willing to step in. The plan is not fragile — it has redundancy built in. Grandparents, extended family, ward members, friends can all fill roles that are missing.

Elder Rasband makes the same point differently: "You have family in your Savior, Jesus Christ." For those whose mortal families have failed them, Christ Himself fills the gap.

**Cross-references**:
- Psalm 68:6 — "God setteth the solitary in families." God's explicit work includes placing the alone into family structures.
- Mosiah 5:7 — "Because of the covenant which ye have made ye shall be called the children of Christ, his sons, and his daughters; for behold, this day he hath spiritually begotten you." Covenant family is real family.

### Theme 4: Technology as False Presence

President Oaks' counsel to "turn your technologies off" is deceptively simple. The deeper insight is that technology creates an *illusion* of connection while actually inserting distance. You can be physically present with your family and spiritually absent — scrolling, distracted, half-listening. The inverse is also true: a family kneeling together in prayer, as President Oaks describes, is technologically unplugged but profoundly connected.

This maps to a broader gospel pattern: Satan's counterfeits often look like the real thing but lack the substance. A social media feed looks like community but lacks covenant. A curated online persona looks like identity but lacks the vulnerability of real relationships.

### Theme 5: The 30-Year Test

Elder Rasband's choice to celebrate the proclamation's 30th anniversary highlights something about prophetic foresight. In 1995, the concerns in the proclamation were often dismissed as alarmist. Thirty years later, declining marriage rates, declining birthrates, and cultural confusion about family roles have confirmed exactly what the document warned about. President Oaks' data on marriage and birthrate decline is the empirical evidence for what the proclamation anticipated.

This raises a question about how we receive prophetic counsel *before* the evidence is obvious. The proclamation was most useful when it was least popular.

## Questions to Wrestle With

### On Family as Doctrine

1. If the family really is the "grammar" of the gospel — the structure everything else operates through — what does that mean for people whose mortal families are broken, abusive, or absent? Is the doctrine cruel to them, or does President Oaks' grandfather story suggest the doctrine is actually *broader* than we think?

2. President Oaks says the gospel plan was "first taught to us in the council of an eternal family." What does it mean that we learned the plan *as a family* before we came here? How should that shape how we teach the plan now?

3. Elder Rasband says the proclamation is "doctrine." President Oaks frames family as the *organizing principle* of the entire plan. If family is that central, why do we sometimes treat it as just one topic among many in our teaching and worship?

### On Compassion and Standards

4. Both speakers hold doctrine firm while extending genuine compassion. In practice, most of us lean one direction or the other — either we soften the doctrine to avoid discomfort, or we hold the standard but lose the warmth. What would it take to hold both simultaneously, the way Rasband and Oaks model?

5. Elder Rasband says "Stay with us... come back." This implies some have left or are leaving. What are the things that push people away, and how many of those are about the doctrine itself versus how we *deliver* the doctrine?

6. President Oaks teaches that spiritual growth continues in the spirit world. If that's true, how should it change how we think about family members who haven't accepted the gospel? Does it make us more patient — or does it risk making us complacent?

### On Practical Family Life

7. "What those children really want for dinner is time with you." If this is true — and it almost certainly is — why is it so hard to act on? What specifically makes us reach for the phone instead of engaging the person in front of us?

8. President Oaks lists specific activities: gardens, camping, sports, yard work, learning languages. These are strikingly ordinary. Is there something about *shared physical work* that binds families in ways that consuming entertainment together does not?

9. Elder Rasband says "preside does not mean dominate" and shares how Sister Rasband corrected him. What does healthy correction look like in a marriage? How do you build a relationship where that kind of feedback is safe?

### On Prophetic Foresight

10. The Family Proclamation was issued in 1995, before many of today's cultural shifts were visible. What current prophetic counsel might we look back on in 30 years and say, "They saw it coming"? Are we paying attention now the way we wish we had then?

---

## Personal Application

1. Elder Rasband emphasized that "preside" does not mean "dominate." How do I ensure equal partnership in my closest relationships?
2. President Oaks said "what those children really want for dinner is time with you." Where am I letting technology substitute for genuine presence?
3. Both speakers extend compassion to those who struggle while affirming doctrine. How do I hold both at the same time in real conversations?
4. President Oaks taught that repentance and growth continue beyond mortality. How does that expand my understanding of God's patience — and should it expand my patience for others?
5. What family traditions, stories, or activities could I start or revive to strengthen the bonds President Oaks described?
